How to Study

Most students don't know the best ways to study. That's not your fault — nobody teaches you! This page covers six strategies backed by decades of research in cognitive science. They actually work.

The big idea

Studying doesn't mean reading your notes over and over. Real studying means doing things with the information — testing yourself, explaining ideas, connecting concepts, and spreading your work across multiple days. The strategies below show you how.

The Six Strategies

These come from research by cognitive scientists. They apply to every subject — history, science, math, languages, anything. Each strategy is something you can start doing right now.

1

Spaced Practice

Spread it out instead of cramming

Instead of studying everything the night before a test, spread your studying across multiple days. Five hours over two weeks beats five hours the night before — every time.

"Five hours spread out over two weeks is better than the same five hours right before the exam."

— The Learning Scientists

Why cramming fails:

  • You forget crammed information fast — sometimes within days
  • Cramming actually takes more total time for the same result
  • Sleep deprivation makes everything worse

What to do instead:

  • Study a little bit each day, starting early
  • Go back and review older material — don't just focus on the newest stuff
  • Even 15 minutes a day is better than 3 hours the night before

2

Retrieval Practice

Test yourself — don't just re-read

The single most powerful study technique: pull information out of your brain instead of just putting more in. Every time you successfully remember something, that memory gets stronger.

"Practicing retrieval makes the information more retrievable later; compared to simply studying by looking over your notes, if you practice retrieval you're more likely to remember the information later, and also more likely to be able to use and apply the information in new situations."

— The Learning Scientists

How to do it:

  • Close your notes and try to write down everything you remember about a topic
  • Use flashcards — but actually try to answer before flipping
  • Take practice tests without looking at your materials
  • After you try to remember, then check your notes to see what you missed

Important: Struggling to remember is actually a good sign. That effort is what makes the memory stronger. If it feels too easy, you're not learning as much.

3

Elaboration

Explain it like you're teaching someone

Don't just memorize a definition — ask yourself how and why it works. Connect new information to things you already know. The more connections you make, the easier it is to remember.

"Explaining and describing ideas with many details... integrate the new material you are learning with the things you already know."

— The Learning Scientists

Try these questions while studying:

  • Why does this work this way?
  • How is this similar to something I already know?
  • What would happen if this were different?
  • Can I explain this to a friend without looking at my notes?

How this tool helps:

Flashcards show simple explanations and real-world examples
Short Answer asks you to connect ideas across topics
Source Analysis asks you to explain why sources matter
4

Interleaving

Mix it up — don't study one thing forever

Instead of studying Topic A for an hour, then Topic B for an hour, switch between them. This feels harder (and it is!) but it forces your brain to practice choosing the right approach, not just following a pattern.

"Switching between ideas while you study... helps you see the links, similarities, and differences between ideas."

— The Learning Scientists

How to do it:

  • Don't spend the whole session on one category — rotate between topics
  • When doing practice problems, mix different types instead of doing 20 of the same kind
  • Go back to earlier material while learning new material

Warning: Don't switch too fast. Spend enough time on each topic to actually think about it before moving on. This isn't multitasking — it's intentional mixing.

5

Concrete Examples

Make abstract ideas real

Abstract ideas are hard to remember. Specific examples make them stick. When you learn a concept, immediately think: "What's a real example of this?"

"Abstract ideas can be vague and hard to grasp... people remember concrete information better than abstract material."

— The Learning Scientists

How to use this:

  • For each vocabulary term, think of a real situation where it applies
  • Look at the examples your teacher provides and make sure you understand why they're examples of that concept
  • Try to come up with your own examples from your life — then check with your teacher that they're accurate
  • The more specific and vivid the example, the better you'll remember it

How this tool helps:

Every flashcard includes a real-world example
Simple explanations connect terms to things you already know
Fill-in-the-Blank puts terms in realistic sentences
6

Dual Coding

Combine words and pictures

When you pair words with visuals — diagrams, timelines, sketches, maps — you create two pathways to the same memory. If you can't remember through one pathway, the other one can help.

"Combining verbal materials with visual materials... when information appears in both word and picture formats, learners gain access to two distinct retrieval pathways during recall."

— The Learning Scientists

How to do it:

  • While studying, draw a quick sketch or diagram to represent key ideas
  • Don't just look at diagrams in your textbook — try to redraw them from memory
  • Create a timeline, flowchart, or mind map connecting your vocabulary terms
  • This works for everyone — it's not just for "visual learners" (that's a myth!)

Common Study Myths

Some popular study advice actually doesn't work. Here's what the research says.

"I'm a visual/auditory learner"

The idea that people learn better when taught in their preferred "style" (visual, auditory, kinesthetic) has been studied extensively — and it doesn't hold up. Everyone benefits from combining words and pictures (dual coding). Everyone benefits from retrieval practice. Don't limit yourself to one approach.

"Re-reading my notes helps me learn"

Re-reading feels productive because the material looks familiar, but familiarity isn't the same as learning. You can recognize something without being able to recall it on a test. Testing yourself is far more effective than re-reading — even if it feels harder.

"Highlighting is studying"

Highlighting doesn't involve any thinking — you're just coloring. It can help you find information later, but it doesn't help you learn it. Instead of highlighting, close your book and try to write down what you remember. That's studying.

"If I study long enough, I'll be ready"

How you study matters more than how long you study. One hour of retrieval practice and elaboration beats three hours of passively re-reading notes. Work smarter, not just longer.
